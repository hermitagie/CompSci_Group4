Charles's Law

History and Background -

This law was named after Jacques-Alexander-Cesar Charles from the 1780s but published by Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac, a French natural philosopher, in 1802.

Definition and Description -

A fundamental principle in physics stating that the volume of an ideal gas is directly proportional to the absolute temperature at constant pressure. The law also states that the Kelvin temperature and the volume will be in direct proportion when the pressure exerted on a sample of a dry gas is held constant.

Example Problem:

A gas is initially at a temperature of 200 K and occupies a volume of 5.0 L. The gas is heated, and its volume expands to 8.0 L. What is the final temperature of the gas, assuming the pressure remains constant?

Initial volume (V1V_1V1​) = 5.0 L
Final volume (V2V_2V2​) = 8.0 L

FINAL ANSWER: T2​=200K×1.6=320K
The final temperature of the gas is 320 K.
